#Here, I'll go ahead and create a function to count the number of nulls in each of my columns

def count_nan(df, num_s):
    list_nans = []
    for i in range(0, num_s):
        nans = df.iloc[: , i].isnull().sum()
        nans_dict = {df.columns.tolist()[i] : nans}
        list_nans.append(nans_dict)
    return list_nans

Conclusions¶

Well, with our first question we saw which directors were the most popular in terms of number of movies. Keep in mind, this set of data has over ten thousand records or movies, but I don't think it's an exhaustive list of all movies ever. So the directors that have put out the highest number of movies in this aggreggation aren't necessarily the most active directors of all time.

Secondly, we saw the correlation in this set between how much was spent on the production of a movie (scaled to 2010 inflation) and how much revenue was gather from it (also scaled to 2010 inflation), and how it wasn't very highly correlated. It is indeed a positive correlation, which was expected, but there was also a lot of variance in the plot. We also saw, on a tangent, that there isn't a very high correlation between our list of directors and the highest grossing movies.

Limitations: As you saw during our data wrangling phase, many of our fields have a number of null values. This could make exploring other, more complex questions a bit difficult, and even if we circumvent that issue there is a chance that it will cause a gap in our results. On top of this issue, there is the face that we do not know exactly how far this list of movies reaches. We could examine the smallest date in the dataframe and assume that the records begin there and go chronologically, but determining whether any movie after that date was missed or omitted would require a lot of cross-checking. Over all, I am quite happy with our results here.
